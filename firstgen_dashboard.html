The First-Generation Student
Achievement Gap

A data-driven look at how family background shapes college access, academic performance, graduation outcomes, and what support programs can do about it.

First-generation college students face compounding barriers — from lower family income to weaker post-graduation earnings — that the system was not built to address. The data below shows where the gaps are largest and why targeted investment in programs like EOF and TRIO matters.

Income & Access
College Continuation Rate by Family Income Quartile
Percentage of students who continue to college, broken down by family income level — illustrating how financial background shapes access to higher education.
Key Finding: Students from the highest income families continue to college at 85% — nearly 30 percentage points higher than students from the lowest income quartile at 56%. Since first-generation students disproportionately come from lower-income households, this gap compounds their disadvantage.

Source: U.S. Census Bureau 1974–2022, compiled by The Pell Institute — Indicators of Higher Education Equity in the United States 2024

Race & First-Generation Status
First-Generation Students by Race/Ethnicity
Percentage of first-generation college students within each racial/ethnic group.
Key Finding: Hispanic/Latino and Black students are most likely to be first-generation college students, at 58% and 51% respectively — nearly double the rate of White students at 30%.

Graduation Outcomes
Graduation Rate by Student Generation
First-generation vs. continuing-generation students. 1977–2017 cohort data.
Only 24% of first-generation students graduate, compared to 59% for continuing-generation students — underscoring the urgent need for mentorship, academic, and financial support programs.
Debt & Income
Student Debt vs. Post-Graduation Income
Average loan debt carried and median income one year after graduation — First-Generation vs. Continuing-Generation students.
Key Finding: First-generation students graduate with $12,000 more debt on average, yet earn $36,000 less per year post-graduation — a compounding financial disadvantage.
EOF Program · 3.0 GPA Threshold
EOF vs. Non-EOF Students Meeting 3.0 GPA — Over Time
Percentage of EOF and non-EOF students maintaining a 3.0+ GPA each academic year, tracking academic excellence over time.
Key Finding: EOF students saw a +13 point jump in 3.0 GPA attainment from 2019 to 2021 — outpacing gains among non-EOF students, indicating that EOF support programs accelerate high academic achievement.

Source: NJ EOF Progress Report 2023

The Solution
Support Programs Close the Gap
Graduation rates across student categories — showing how EOF and TRIO support programs dramatically lift first-generation student outcomes.
Key Finding: First-generation students without support graduate at just 24%. With TRIO SSS that rises to 42%, and with EOF to 58% — nearly matching the 59% continuing-generation rate. Targeted programs work.

Sources:
FirstGen Forward 2024  ·  U.S. Dept of Education 2019  ·  NJ EOF Progress Report 2023

For first-generation and low-income students, programs like EOF and TRIO aren't just academic resources — they are often the first time these students have access to a college counselor, a financial aid advisor, or a mentor who looks like them and understands where they come from. These programs open doors that were never part of the plan: tutoring, emergency funding, career networking, and a community that makes staying in school feel possible. Investing in them isn't a bonus — it's the bridge that turns potential into completion.

Our future leaders should be well-educated, driven, and ready to contribute — and higher education is the single most powerful vehicle to make that happen for every student, regardless of where they started.
